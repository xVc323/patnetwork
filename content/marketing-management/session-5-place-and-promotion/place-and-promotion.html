1. Place

Place?

Definition

Place: the process of moving products from the producer to the intended user… in other words, it is how a product is bought and where it is bought #place

Delivery Model

Channel Structure

Definition

Channel Structure: Refers to the path a product takes from producer to consumer. #channel-structure

  • Different delivery models exist in every industry
  • Range from short (i.e., direct) to long
  • Different types may be combined (i.e., multichannel approach)


Level 0: Direct from producer to customer (e.g., online stores, direct mail).

Level 1: Producer to retailer/platform to customer (e.g., department stores, online marketplaces).

Level 2: Producer to wholesaler/distributor to retailer to customer (e.g., FMCG products).

Level 3: Producer to agent/broker to wholesaler/distributor to retailer to customer (e.g., complex products).

Examples


Level 0 (Direct Channel): Apple selling MacBooks directly to customers via its website.

Level 1 (One-Level Channel): Apple selling iPhones through retailers like FNAC and Amazon to customers.

Level 2 (Two-Level Channel): P&G products distributed by McKesson to retailers like Remedy’sRx, then to customers.

Level 4 (Agent/Broker Channel):
Example: Van der Does Spice Brokers:

  • Structure: Manufacturer → Agent/Broker → Wholesaler/Distributor → Retailer → Customers
  • Description: Van der Does Spice Brokers acts as an intermediary, adding value to the supply chain by providing services like auditing, negotiation, and contract management.

What factors impact the length of the channel structure?

  • In what circumstances is longer “better”?
  • In what circumstances is shorter “better”?


Producer Factors: Producers with adequate resources, broad product lines, and a need for channel control may prefer shorter channels.

Competitive Factors: Manufacturers satisfied with the performance of their marketing intermediaries may opt for longer channels.

Retail Partner Selection

Definition

Retailing: includes all the activities involved in selling goods or services directly to final consumers for their personal, non-business use. #retailing

Various formats (online & offline):

  • speciality stores (i.e., Decathlon)
  • supermarkets (i.e., Intermarché)
  • discount stores (i.e., Action)
  • superstores/hypermarkets (i.e., Auchan)
  • department stores (i.e., Galeries Lafayette)
  • convenience stores (i.e., Spar)
  • off-price retailers (i.e., GAP)
  • online retailers/marketplaces (i.e., Amazon)

Producer Considerations

#producer

Product Availability:

  • Distribution intensity (intensive, selective, exclusive)
  • In-store positioning (shelf-facings, occupation rate)

Promotional Ability:

  • Retailer's ability to execute point-of-purchase promotions
  • Collaborative campaigns.

Cost-Effectiveness:

  • Transportation cost
  • Inventory costs
  • Middlemen margins
  • Retailer investments.

Customer Considerations: Retailer image, target customer shopping habits, and brand equity alignment.

  • Where do customer shop?
  • What criteria do they find important for (online) store choice? Level of brand equity?

Retailer Considerations

#retailer

Revenue Potential:

Investment Requirements:

  • Investment needed to support the product intro
  • Investments in producer relationship: IT systems, delivery systems,…
  • Investments provided by the producer: POS-investment (Point of Sale),…

Assortment Competition:

  • Fit with existing product assortment
  • Competition with private labels
  • Producer/Brand Equity

Producer-Retailer conflicts may happen…

Channel conflict exists when channel members interfere with each others’ objectives:

  • Horizontal conflict involves firms on same level – e.g., grocery store vs drug store
  • Vertical conflict involves firms at different levels – e.g., producer vs wholesaler, producer vs retailer

Channel power is the ability to influence or determine behavior of others in channel (based on expertise, rewards, sanctions, size of business…)

In-store place decisions

This is an objective strongly linked to the job of category managers
→ i.e., people tasked to grow the overall revenue generated for a specific category for a retailer; work both at the retail and producer side.

Key decision 1: in what aisle do we put the product(s)?
Key decision 2: Where in the aisle do we put the product(s)?

Planogram

Definition

It is a visual representation providing details as to where a product should be placed on a shelf and how many facings a product should have. Tool used by retailers and supplier alike. #planogram #perceptual-maps

Retailer goal: optimize shelf space in order to increase sales.

Supplier goal: justify space allocation to brands and help new product development.

Special Places in Store

Special product placements can enhance visibility and sales:

  1. End-cap Displays: Located at the end of aisles to attract attention.
  2. Check-out Placement: Positioned near the register for impulse purchases.
  3. Entry Aisle Promotion Zones: Placed at the store entrance to catch customers' attention as they enter.


Placement Fees

The fees a food manufacturer pays for placement of its product anywhere in a store, wether by the register, on a endcap (an end-of-aisle display), by the deli, or on a shelf.

Display fees: payments for premium spots like an endcap, cardboard displays that retailers call “shippers”, or the “shelf talker” signs on shelf facings that call attention to a product.

Pay-to-Stay fees: payments manufacturers must make to remain on a store’s shelf. These are not necessarily cash payments; often manufacturers compensate retailers in the form of free merchandise and reduced prices.

Slotting fees[1]: the fees manufacturers pay to introduce a new product on a store’s shelf.

New Platforms

Omnichannel Retailing

Omnichannel retailing integrates multiple channels (online and offline) to enhance customer experience and sales, creating synergies rather than cannibalizing channels.

The power of platform models

Types of Platform Business Models:

  • Social Media Platforms: Facebook, LinkedIn, Twitter
  • Communication Platforms: WhatsApp, Skype, WeChat, LINE
  • Search Platforms: Google, Bing, Yahoo, Safari
  • Sharing Economy Platforms: Airbnb, Uber, ZipCar
    • Peer-to-peer sharing
    • Platform owner-provided assets
    • Capacity-unconstrained resources (e.g., file sharing)
  • Matching Platforms: TaskRabbit, Tinder, e-Harmony
  • Content and Review Platforms: YouTube, TripAdvisor, Yelp
  • Booking Platforms: Booking.com, Expedia, Pagoda
  • Retail Platforms: Amazon, Etsy, eBay, Craigslist
  • Payment Platforms: PayPal, Alipay, Visa
  • Development Platforms: App stores, gaming consoles
  • Crowdsourcing and Crowdfunding Platforms: InnoCentive, Kickstarter

Platforms models

  1. Quadrant 1:

    • Description: Marketer-provided resources with a transfer of ownership.
    • Example: Traditional retail platforms where products are sold directly to consumers.

  2. Quadrant 2:

    • Description: Peer-to-peer-provided resources with a transfer of ownership.
    • Example: Marketplaces like eBay, where individuals sell their own goods to other individuals.

  3. Quadrant 3:

    • Description: Marketer-provided resources with access-based use; no transfer of ownership.
    • Example: Subscription services like Netflix, where content is provided by the company but only accessed by users.

  4. Quadrant 4:

    • Description: Peer-to-peer-provided resources with access-based use; no transfer of ownership.
    • Example: Sharing economy platforms like Airbnb, where individuals rent out their properties to others without transferring ownership

Promotion involves using communication to foster customer engagement behaviors such as buying or word-of-mouth. It includes a wide range of activities aimed at influencing the target audience's perceptions and actions. #promotion

Market

Effective communication strategies depend on understanding the target audience. Identifying and selecting customer segments is crucial for tailoring messages and choosing the right media. #market

Check 1: Who do you want to reach? (i.e., target segment(s))
Check 2: Where can you find these people (i.e., based on consumer profiling).

Mission

#mission
The mission defines the specific goals of the communication campaign.

Goals:

  • Attracting new customers
  • Winning back lost customers
  • Encouraging trials
  • Showcasing product/service use
  • Encouraging brand switching
  • Creating a brand image
  • Reminding customers
  • Highlighting brand position
  • Stimulating extra sales
  • Lowering the cost of sales
  • Enhancing brand loyalty
  • Reducing cognitive dissonance

Goal Statement:

  • selected target audience #targeting
  • definition of impact on audience
  • timeline of impact
  • degree of change required

Example Statement: increase awarness of the 3-year warranty on all of our products to 50% of the population by June 1, 2019.

2) Message Structure & Format

How to structure and format the message (informational vs. transformational appeal, use of humor, sex appeal, fear appeal).

Informational Appeal:

  • Gives a reason to buy that is practical or functional
  • e.g., product quality, economic value, performance claims, technical attributes

Transformational Appeal:

  • Tries to stir up positive or negative emotions to motivate engagement
  • e.g., fear, guilt, sex, shame, love, humor, pride, joy…

Humor appeal:

  • Attracts attention
  • Entertains customers
  • May enhance word-of-mouth
  • Yet, NO clear impact on purchase behavior

Sex appeal

Sex appeals in advertising can be found in:

  • Physical features of models (clothing, physique and general attractiveness)
  • Behavior/movement
  • Intimacy between models
  • Contextual features

Gratuitous use of sex in advertising:

  • Ads with sexual appeal are remembered more than ads without it (due to higher attention and message processing, which facilitates message retrieval)
    BUT no significant positive/negative impact on the brand itself!
  • No extra impact on purchase intentions compared to ads without sexual appeal.
  • An unapropriate fit (e.g., sexual appeal for a tool set, USB drive, breakfast cereal) leads to negative outcomes.
  • Sexual appeal works better for men than it does for women -> higher ad liking.

Fear appeal

Fear appeals are successful at influencing:

  • attitudes
  • intentions
  • behaviors

They work better for women than men and they work better for one-time (e.g., getting vaccinated) versus repeated behaviors (e.g., exercising multiple times per week every week)

3) Message source

Who delivers the message (expert, celebrity, influencer, typical consumer).

Some checks:

  • Expertise: does the communication possess (specialized) knowledge to back the claim?
  • Trustworthiness: how objective and honest is the source perceived to be?
  • Likability: how attractive is the source?

Typical choice:

  • Expert (e.g., doctor dentist…)
  • Attractive spokesperson (celebrity)
  • Influencer (online – social media)
  • “Typical” consumer

Celebrities

Celebrity endorsements can positively impact attitudes & behaviors under certain conditions.

  • Celebrity – product match is necessary (e.g., attractive actor/actress – perfume)
  • Male endorses > female endorsers
  • Implicit endorsements > explicit endorsements
  • Actors > models, musicians, TV hosts
  • Endorsements of unfamiliar object > familiar object
  • Quality seals, awards > celebrity endorsement

Influencers

Celebrity influencer: brand awareness

Mega influencer: brand awareness

Macro influencer: product consideration, purchase intent

Micro influencer: product consideration, purchase intent

Nano influencer: word-of-mouth and engagement within a specific niche

Who to adopt as influencer for your campaign?

Influencers can significantly boost brand awareness, product consideration, and engagement. Key considerations for influencer marketing include:

  1. Relevance of the influencer – i.e., fit with your product? fit with your audience?
  2. Reach of the influencer – i.e., how much people can you reach?
  3. Resonance of the influencer – i.e., what is the level of engagement that can be created?
  4. What is the ‘trust-level’ of the influencer - i.e., how well is the influencer perceived by their audience?
  5. What is the sponsorship saturation of the influencer? – i.e. how often does the influencer promote products? which ones?
  6. What budget is involved? What is needed to bring the influencer on board?
  7. How much creative freedom do you want to give to the influencer?
  8. Transparency of the influencer? – i.e., openness on being sponsored? (#ad, #sponsored)

Media

Selecting the right media channels is crucial for reaching the target audience effectively. The Media Trifecta includes:

  1. Paid Media: Advertising through paid channels (e.g., TV ads, social media ads).
  2. Owned Media: Channels controlled by the brand (e.g., website, social media pages).
  3. Earned Media: Publicity gained through word-of-mouth or organic mentions (e.g., reviews, shares).

Channel Selection:

  • Consider target audience media consumption.
  • Align media choices with campaign goals.
  • Evaluate affordability and cost-effectiveness of each channel.

Digital Advertising: A rapidly growing segment of the advertising market, encompassing search advertising, social media advertising, banner advertising, video advertising, and classifieds.




Checklist:
✅ What media is my target population frequenting?
✅ What media fit my campaign goal(s) best?
✅ What media can I afford and is cost-effective?

Money

A budget needs to be determined

Fixed percentage of sales

  • This approach assumes that sales are directly related to advertising
  • If the rule is applied when sales are declining…?

Task-based

  • Setting marketing objectives based on the “tasks” that the advertising has to complete

Residual-based

  • To base the advertising budget on what the business can afford – after all other expenditure

Same level as competitors (→ see benchmark reports)

  • When products are well-established with predictable sales patterns
  • It could also prevent a business with competitive advantages from increasing market share by spending more than average

Planning is crucial!

Our estimates on time-of-day effects suggest that, ceteris paribus, posting stories in the morning generates approximately an 8.8% (11.1%) increase in link clicks compared to posting stories in the afternoon (evening). Assuming that page depth is 1 and the cost per impression is $0.06 (the lowest observed return among the seven categories), this 8.8% (11.1%) increase translates into a gross profit of $25,529 ($32,201) for a content platform that posts 5,000 free stories per year.

Theory? Higher availability of working memory in the morning → higher levels of alertness, curiosity, and information seeking.
As the day continues → stress increases → working memory availability diminishes → less desire to engage with social media content.

Measurement

Evaluating the success of a promotional campaign requires specific metrics.

Advertising Metrics

1. Impressions (exposures – opportunities-to-see (OTS))

  • Purpose: Understand how many times an ad is viewed
    #impression

2. Gross Rating Points (GRPs)

  • Purpose: Measure impressions in relation to the number of people in the audience
    #GRP

3. Cost per Thousand Impressions (CPM)

  • Purpose: Measure cost-effectiveness of impression generation
    #CPM

4. Net Reach

  • Purpose: Measure breadth of an ad campaign

5. Average Frequency

  • Purpose: Measure how strongly an ad is concentrated on a given population

6. Effective Reach

  • Purpose: Measure portion of an audience that is exposed to an ad enough times to be influenced
  • *A frequency of 3 is considered as a rule of thumb

7. Clickthrough Rate (CTR)

  • Purpose: Measure effectiveness of a web ad by counting how many are intrigued to click on it
    #CTR

8. Cost per Click (CPC)

  • Purpose: Measure or establish the cost-effectiveness of advertising
    #cpc
